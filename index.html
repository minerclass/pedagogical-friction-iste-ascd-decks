Two ways of framing the problem 03

A policy question, or a question of consciousness.

Framing A · Tool adoption

AI is a productivity tool. Manage it through policy: integrity, data privacy, vendor review, acceptable use.

This is where most district AI policy lives today.

Framing B · Media ecology

AI is an environment. Each new medium restructures how children remember, attend, reason, and know.

This is the framing K-12 has not yet adopted — and the one this talk argues we need.

PART 01 · MEDIA ECOLOGY

Stages of the symbolic environment.

Each new medium reorganizes the cognition that lives inside it. K-12 is now negotiating the fifth such transition — and the first in which the content itself is generated by machines.
Walter Ong · post-Ong media ecology 05 / 22
The developmental account, extended 06

Five stages, not three.

Ong gave us oral, literate, and broadcast. The current moment requires two further stages — one for algorithmic distribution, one for algorithmic generation.

STAGE 01
Primary orality
Memory-based, communal, formulaic. Knowledge survives by repetition.
PRE-LITERATE
STAGE 02

Writing externalizes memory. Analytical, abstract, individual interiority.

RADIO · TV
STAGE 04 NEW
Algorithmic secondary orality
Humans still author; algorithms decide what reaches whom. Feeds, not channels.
SOCIAL · 2005–
STAGE 05 NEW
Tertiary algorithmicity
Machines both curate and generate. Human authorship becomes optional at scale.
GENAI · 2022–

A K-12 classroom in 2026 is operating across all five at once.

Section II · Theoretical framework 06 / 22
Why we need a new stage at all 07

Three assumptions that no longer hold.

Ong's framework rested on three things we can no longer take for granted.

01
Humans originate the symbolic content.
Neural networks now produce essays, summaries, analyses, images without human authorship in any traditional sense.
02
Distribution follows transparent logics.
Two students opening the same app see different content — curated by opaque, engagement-optimizing systems.
03
Consciousness encounters media as external.
Personalized environments increasingly reflect a child's prior behavior back — removing the disequilibrium learning requires.

Section III · Proposed extensions 08 / 22
What's new about this stage, specifically 09

Three characteristics of the new stage.

01
Noetic displacement
The cognitive labor of meaning-making — interpretation, composition, synthesis — can be performed by a system that has never had an experience.
02
Rhetorical saturation
Plausible, fluent prose floods every channel a student touches. The signal of "competent thought" is no longer a reliable indicator of thought.
03
Existential abstraction
The link between a claim and the person making it dissolves. Authorship — and accountability for ideas — becomes ambient.

Section IV · Educational stakes 12 / 22
A common mistake in K-12 framing 13

This is not, primarily, an academic integrity problem.

The integrity frame

Locates the problem in a few students who cheat. Solves it with detection tools, honor codes, locked browsers.

Necessary, but insufficient — and aimed at the wrong target.

The developmental frame

Locates the problem in the media environment itself, whose default tendency is the bypass of cognitive labor.

Students aren't lazy. The path of least resistance now leads to unproductive success.

Section IV · Educational stakes 13 / 22
What's at risk inside the K-12 classroom 14

Three losses, K-12 specific.

01
Attention & the sensorium
Children develop inside feeds that reward rapid consumption over deliberation, emotional response over critical evaluation. Tertiary algorithmicity adds generated content to this — endlessly, fluently.
02
Knowledge & interpretation
Each prior media stage externalized memory but kept generation human. This stage externalizes the production of knowledge-like discourse itself. Students must now evaluate claims of uncertain origin.
03
Agency & authorship
Creative agency becomes distributed between child and system. Epistemic agency confronts origin uncertainty. Dialogic agency meets simulated interlocutors that never disagree in productive ways.

PART 03 · A RESPONSE FRAMEWORK

Pedagogical friction.

Not a call to make school harder. A call to protect the specific difficulties that learning depends on — when the default of the medium is to remove them.
Section V · Pedagogical friction 15 / 22
Definition 16
Pedagogical friction (n.)
The intentional preservation of the cognitive, dialogic, and existential resistance that durable learning requires — when frictionless automation has become the default condition of the medium.
In physics, friction is energy lost. In pedagogy, it's the energy through which development occurs.
Section V · Pedagogical friction 16 / 22
The framework, four columns 17

Four dimensions of friction.

Three phenomenological, one structural. Together they specify what K-12 has to protect.

The Head

Noetic friction

The internal struggle of comprehension, synthesis, and revising one's own thinking. Where schemas actually get built.

Threat · BypassThe model writes the analysis. The schema is never constructed.
The Room

Rhetorical friction

The dialogic struggle of defending claims to unpredictable human interlocutors. Discussion, peer review, oral defense.

Threat · SimulationAI provides fluent reply without genuine disagreement.
The World

Existential friction

The vulnerability of being held personally accountable for one's own thinking, in physical space, in front of others.

Threat · AbstractionAuthor and claim come unbound. Risk vanishes.
The System

Infrastructural friction

District policy, assessment design, prof. dev., the values communicated through institutional practice.

Threat · SilenceMost current AI policy is mute on learning science.
Section V · Pedagogical friction 17 / 22
The equity guardrail on the framework 18

Not all friction is productive.

"Preserve struggle" without this distinction is a recipe for reproducing inequity under the language of rigor.

Preserve →

Productive friction

  • Wrestling with a difficult text
  • Defending an argument under critique
  • Composing original work from one's own understanding
  • Revising in response to feedback
  • Retrieving without notes; explaining without prompts
Reduce →

Exclusionary friction

  • Language barriers imposed on content assessment
  • Inaccessible formats for students with disabilities
  • Procedural requirements unrelated to learning objectives
  • Tests of stamina misread as tests of understanding
  • Norms that treat one cognitive profile as the default
Section V · Pedagogical friction 18 / 22
A K-12 case the framework has to handle 19

The English Learner paradox.

A multilingual student uses an AI translator to convert a draft from their first language into English. What just happened?

Access gained

An exclusionary barrier just dropped.

The student can demonstrate content knowledge and join academic discourse without being filtered through a language barrier unrelated to the learning objective.

Voice lost

Authorship may quietly flatten.

Translation can produce grammatically correct, stylistically generic prose — removing the perspective and phrasing that make a child's writing recognizably their own.

No mechanical rule resolves this. The same tool reduces both kinds of friction at once. Distinguishing them requires situated, professional judgment — which is what the framework asks K-12 educators to develop.

Section V.D · Implications for K-12 20 / 22
Three columns of moves 21

Curriculum, advocacy, policy.

Friction has to become a structural value, not a personal one — or it will not survive.

Curriculum

Make thinking visible.

  • Assess process, not only product
  • Sequence: noetic struggle before AI assistance
  • Bring back oral defense, peer critique, in-class drafting
  • Require reflection on how a piece was made
  • Design assignments AI cannot complete in one prompt
Advocacy

Name friction as a protected value.

  • Push back on "efficiency" as the only frame
  • Speak from technology leadership, not against it
  • Equip teachers to articulate why struggle matters
  • Defend the time productive struggle requires
  • Bring families into the conversation early
Policy

Close the learning-science gap.

  • District AI policy must reference cognition, not only risk
  • Distinguish productive from exclusionary use cases
  • Build assessment that values process
  • Fund prof. dev. on situated judgment, not tool training
  • Audit policies for equity blind spots — DisCrit lens
